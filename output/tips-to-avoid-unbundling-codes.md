What is Unbundling Codes?
-------------------------

Unbundling codes in medical billing refers to the practice of separately billing for individual components of a medical procedure or service, rather than billing for the complete procedure or service as a single entity. This practice can result in higher payments to healthcare providers and higher costs for patients and insurers.

**For example**, imagine a patient receives a single medical service that includes a group of tests. If the provider bills for each test separately rather than billing for the single service that includes all of the tests, this is considered unbundling. Unbundling can be deliberate or unintentional. Deliberate unbundling is fraudulent and illegal, while unintentional unbundling may occur due to errors in coding or billing.

The Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS) has developed specific guidelines to prevent unbundling and ensure accurate coding and billing practices. These guidelines include instructions for when it is appropriate to bill for bundled services and when it is necessary to bill for individual components separately.

Unbundling codes can have significant financial implications for patients and insurers. It is important for healthcare providers to accurately bill for services to ensure fair and appropriate compensation and to avoid potential legal and financial penalties for fraudulent billing practices.

Tips to Avoid Unbundling Codes
------------------------------

Unbundling codes could result in incorrect or excessive reimbursement and is considered fraudulent. Here are some tips to avoid unbundling of codes:

### 1\. Use Comprehensive Codes

To avoid unbundling codes, healthcare providers should use comprehensive codes that accurately reflect the full range of services provided. Let’s discuss an example of how a healthcare provider might use comprehensive codes to avoid unbundling codes.

A patient comes in for a comprehensive physical exam, which includes a routine check-up, a complete blood count (CBC), and a lipid panel. Instead of billing for each service separately, the provider would use a comprehensive code that encompasses all of these services.

For example, they might use the code 99385, which represents a comprehensive preventive medicine evaluation and management of a patient including an age and gender-appropriate history, examination, counseling/anticipatory guidance/risk factor reduction interventions, and the ordering of laboratory/diagnostic procedures, established patient; 18-39 years of age.

By using a comprehensive code like this, the provider can ensure that they are billing for the full range of services provided, without risking potential unbundling issues. It also helps to simplify the billing process and avoid confusion for both the provider and the patient.

### 2\. Understand National Correct Coding Initiative (NCCI) Edits

Make sure you understand the codes and their components before reporting them. Familiarize yourself with the National Correct Coding Initiative (NCCI) edits that provide guidance on which codes can and cannot be billed together.

The National Correct Coding Initiative (NCCI) is a program developed by the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services ([CMS](https://cms.gov/)) to prevent improper payment of Medicare claims. NCCI edits are sets of codes that are used to determine if a procedure or service is appropriate to be billed together.

The NCCI edits contain pairs of codes, referred to as ‘Column One’ and ‘Column Two’ codes. The Column One code is the primary code or procedure being performed, while the Column Two code is a secondary code or procedure that is typically not performed separately from the Column One code.

When a claim is submitted with both a Column One and Column Two code, the NCCI edits will check to see if the codes are allowed to be billed together. If the codes are not allowed to be billed together, the NCCI edits will reject the claim, and the provider will need to submit a corrected claim.

For example, if provider bills for both a lumbar puncture (Column One code) and a spinal tap (Column Two code), the NCCI edits will reject the claim because these two procedures are considered to be the same procedure and cannot be billed separately.

It’s important for healthcare providers and billing staff to be familiar with the NCCI edits to ensure that claims are submitted correctly and avoid potential penalties or fines for improper billing.
