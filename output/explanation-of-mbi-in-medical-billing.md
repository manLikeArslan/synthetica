# Explanation of MBI in Medical Billing

*Original URL: https://docsmedicalbilling.com/explanation-of-mbi-in-medical-billing/*

---

The Medicare Beneficiary Identifier streamlines the electronic or manual claim submissions. It enables providers and RCM vendors to file claims separately for every patient and service rendered. The provider’s document claims to accept payments from Medicare by using the patient’s MBI in medical billing. In addition, the provider sends these identifiers to streamline the billing process when **[outsourcing medical billing services](https://docsmedicalbilling.com/medical-billing-and-coding-services/)**. MBI is used as the primary beneficiary identifier on Medicare fee-for-service claims instead of the former SSN-based Health Insurance Claim Number (HICN).

It is an 11-digit Medicare Beneficiary Identifier for patients receiving medical coverage from Medicare. More precisely, it is an 11-character alphanumeric identifier (a mix of numbers and uppercase letters) assigned to each Medicare beneficiary. Medicare has removed ICN in healthcare billing and implemented MBI. Medicare actually replaced the SSN-based Health Insurance Claim Number (HICN) with the MBI as the beneficiary identifier on Medicare cards and claims.  
Likewise, Medicare patients use MBI instead of the Insurance Claim Number. The old HICN included the beneficiary’s Social Security Number with a suffix, which is no longer used on new Medicare cards. In addition, ICN is a social security number that Medicare doesn’t accept as an identifier number to receive medical services and their reimbursement. Instead of the SSN-based HICN, Medicare now requires the MBI on most claims and eligibility transactions.

Medicare Access and CHIP Reauthorization Act of 2015 (MACRA) denies the use of Social Security Number (SSN) based HICN to secure data and patients’ personal information. As HICN is derived from SSN, it increases the risk of exposing personal information. Therefore, MBI in medical billing was designed to protect patients from identity theft because it is comprised of randomly selected, meaningless characters. These characters follow a specific CMS-defined pattern but are “non-intelligent,” meaning they do not encode SSNs, dates of birth, or other personal details.

The HICN (Health Insurance Claim Number) was SSN-based and was phased out for most Medicare fee-for-service billing by the end of 2019, while the MBI became mandatory for almost all claims starting January 1, 2020.

MBI in medical billing has no impact on the Medicare benefits. People with Medicare Advantage plans or Medicare Drug Plan cards continue to receive medical services as they always have. Similarly, providers examine and treat patients and bill claims to Medicare based on their assigned Medicare identifier to receive payments. The change to MBIs affects how beneficiaries and providers identify the patient on Medicare transactions, not the scope of covered services. They are designed specifically so that no portion of the MBI can be interpreted to reveal a beneficiary’s SSN, age, or other personal attributes.

It lacks meaning because the characters are randomly selected to generate the Medicare identifier. According to the Centers for Medicare and Medicaid instructions, these non-intelligent characters in MBI have no hidden or special meaning. They are designed specifically so that no portion of the MBI can be interpreted to reveal a beneficiary’s SSN, age, or other personal attributes.

Identification in medical billing is crucial for streamlining the billing operations and reimbursement. Similarly, to enhance data security, Medicare assigns an identification number to every individual. By using MBI in medical billing, providers file claims with accurate documentation to the payer and receive reimbursement. Hence, clean claim submission with MBI accuracy is necessary to receive timely payments.
