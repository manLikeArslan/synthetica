# How to Read a Blue Cross Blue Shield Insurance Card

*Original URL: https://docsmedicalbilling.com/how-to-read-a-blue-cross-blue-shield-insurance-card/*

---

Reading a Blue Cross Blue Shield (BCBS) insurance card correctly is one of the most critical skills for healthcare providers and medical billing professionals. According to the American Medical Association (AMA), approximately 10-20% of insurance claims are denied due to administrative or billing errors, with many stemming from incorrect patient information pulled directly from insurance cards. For medical practices, this translates to thousands of dollars in lost revenue and countless hours spent on claim resubmissions and appeals.

Before we examine the insurance card itself, it’s important to understand that Blue Cross Blue Shield is not a single insurance company but rather a federation of 35 independent Blue Cross and Blue Shield companies operating across all 50 states. Each regional BCBS company operates independently, which means insurance cards may vary slightly in design and layout depending on the state and specific plan.

The Member ID number, also called the Subscriber ID or Policy Number, is the unique identifier assigned to the primary policyholder by Blue Cross Blue Shield. This alphanumeric code typically contains 9-12 characters and may include a prefix that identifies the specific BCBS plan or region.

The Member ID number is the most critical piece of information on the insurance card. Every insurance claim, eligibility verification, and benefits inquiry require this number. Entering an incorrect Member ID is one of the most common causes of claim denials in medical billing.

The Group Number identifies the employer or organization that sponsors the insurance plan. This number is essential for group policies, which are typically provided by employers to their employees and dependents.

The Group Number determines the specific benefits package, coverage levels, and contracted rates for services. Different groups under the same BCBS plan may have different coverage terms, co-pays, and deductibles. Billing without the correct Group Number can result in claim denials or incorrect payment amounts.

If there’s no Group Number on the card, the patient has an individual plan purchased through the Health Insurance Marketplace, directly from BCBS, or through Medicare/Medicaid. These plans are billed differently and may have different coverage rules.

The name of the primary policyholder (subscriber) appears on the front of the card. If the patient is a dependent (spouse, child, domestic partner), their name may also appear on the card, typically below the subscriber’s name or on a separate dependent card.

Accurate name verification prevents claim denials due to name mismatches and helps ensure you’re billing the correct insurance policy. It’s also essential for patient identification and HIPAA compliance.

The plan type indicates the structure of the patient’s health insurance coverage and determines important factors like whether referrals are required, which providers are in-network, and how much patients pay for out-of-network care.

The effective date shows when the patient’s insurance coverage began. Some cards also show an expiration date, though many policies renew automatically annually.

Verifying the effective date ensures the patient has active coverage at the time of service. Providing care to a patient whose coverage hasn’t started yet or has lapsed can result in denied claims and difficulty collecting payment from the patient.

Patients who recently switched jobs or changed plans may present an old card even though their coverage has changed. Always verify eligibility rather than relying solely on the card.

Co-pays, deductibles, and coinsurance represent the patient’s financial responsibility for healthcare services. These amounts vary by plan and service type.

The amount the patient must pay out-of-pocket before insurance coverage begins. Once the deductible is met, the insurance company starts paying its share. Annual deductibles commonly range from $500 to $5,000 for individual plans.

The percentage of the cost that the patient pays after meeting their deductible. For example, if the coinsurance is 20%, the patient pays 20% of the allowed amount and insurance pays 80%. Coinsurance applies until the patient reaches their out-of-pocket maximum.
