# How well do you know your EOB?

*Original URL: https://www.medicalbillersandcoders.com/blog/how-well-do-you-know-your-eob/*

---

  

EOB is an outcome of the claims process and is alternatively termed as a Beneficiary Notice, Summary of Benefits, Remittance Advisory, or Coverage Determination.

EOB is NOT A BILL though it looks like one. It is simply an explanation of the claim covered/not covered by the healthcare insurers to the healthcare provider along with any details of the patient being responsible for a sum of money left to be paid. It is sent on a monthly basis unless asked otherwise (via an online platform).

Components of an EOB:
---------------------

1.  **Enrollee name:** This section mentions the name of the insurance policyholder.
2.  **Patient name:** This section mentions the name of the patient who could be the enrollee himself or other family members (grouped under the enrollee’s name).
3.  **Patient number:** An identification number of the patient.
4.  **Enrollee address:** This section contains the address and contact number of the policyholder.
5.  **Type of service/Products:** This column lists the services/products that the patient has made use of from a healthcare provider.
6.  **HCPCS/CPT/[ICD-10](https://www.medicalbillersandcoders.com/ICD-10.aspx)/Revenue codes:** EOB could have any/all of these codes as per the services/products rendered by the patient.
7.  **Charge amount/Claim:** This section mentions the amount charged by the healthcare provider.
8.  **Claim number:** A number in the computer system by which the patient and his/her claim are identified.
9.  **Date and place of service:** The date and place on which the service was rendered.
10.  **Costing/charges covered:** This section mentions the costs paid by the insurer for each service to the healthcare provider.
11.  **Payment amount/Benefit amount:** The total amount paid by the insurance provider along with the benefits (percentage of the amount) paid.
12.  **Ineligible amount/Reason code:** Along with a code, this column states the charges and the reasons due to which they have not been covered in the claim.
13.  **Other insurances:** This section mentions the amount paid by an additional insurance provider.
14.  **The amount owed by the patient:** In case the patient hasn’t paid yet, then this charge is the patient’s responsibility: co-insurance, copay, deductible, and/or non-covered services.
15.  **Process date:** Date on which the insurance provider finalized its assessment on the claim.

The healthcare provider’s and the insurance provider’s information is also mentioned on EOB. The patient must ensure that he/she looks through the EOB thoroughly once received. Checking for the above-mentioned points ensures that you are not being billed incorrectly for a service/product not rendered. You are also informed of your copay or deductibles.

In case of any error, contact your physician or the insurance provider to clear doubts. Keep your EOB statements up till a year and review your EOB carefully every time you receive it to avoid fraud and overcharging.

**FAQs**
--------

****1\. What is an Explanation of Benefits (EOB), and how is it different from a bill?****

An EOB is not a bill; it is a summary document provided by insurance companies that explains the claims processed, including amounts covered, denied, or remaining for the patient to pay.

****2\. What details are included in an EOB?****

An EOB includes information such as the enrollee and patient names, service dates, claim numbers, services/products rendered, HCPCS/CPT/ICD-10 codes, amounts covered by the insurer, ineligible amounts with reason codes, and the amount owed by the patient.

****3\. What should a patient do after receiving an EOB?****

Patients should thoroughly review their EOB to ensure all services and charges are accurate. If any discrepancies are found, they should contact their physician or insurance provider for clarification.

****4\. Why is it important to keep EOB statements?****

EOB statements should be retained for at least a year to help review past claims, verify payments, and protect against fraud or overcharging.
