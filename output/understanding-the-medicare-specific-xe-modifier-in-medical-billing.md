Modifier XE was introduced by the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid in 2022, along with three other new HCPCS modifiers. It applies when a provider gives services to a patient on the same day at separate encounters. Although the XE modifier in medical billing is often confused with modifier 59, there is a difference between both modifiers. XE is the HCPCS modifier, and 59 is set by the American Medical Association (AMA). Furthermore, the accurate usage of these modifiers enables providers to receive rightful reimbursement from the payer. Likewise, providers can [**outsource medical billing services**](https://docsmedicalbilling.com/medical-billing-and-coding-services/) to reduce the risk of using the wrong modifiers. This article explains XE modifiers in detail.

What is an XE Modifier in Medical Billing?
------------------------------------------

In 2022, CMS introduced a few HCPCS modifiers, which are XE, XP, XS, and XU. These modifiers apply under different circumstances, but they describe separate encounters. The billing staff applies modifier XE when a patient receives distinct medical services on the same day but at different times. For example, a patient comes for radiotherapy treatment (**[CPT Code 77427](https://docsmedicalbilling.com/ultimate-guide-to-cpt-code-77427/)**) in the morning. Later that day, the same patient came for abnormal heart rhythm and received a rhythm ECG (CPT Code 93040). Hence, the healthcare staff files a claim for the medical services rendered in the following way:

*   Column 1 Code: 77427
*   Column 2 Code: 93040- XE

Difference Between Modifiers XE and 59
--------------------------------------

Modifier 59 and XE are confusing because they almost relate to each other. The major difference is that 59 is of the American Medical Association, and XE is of HCPCS. Modifier 59 explains only distinct non-E/M (Evaluation/Management) services a patient receives on the same day but at different times. Most payers accept it.  
XE Modifier in medical billing is used for two separate encounters for different services on the same day but at different times. Similarly, it typically reports non-E/M services. It is a Medicare-specific modifier, and private payers may reject it. In addition, it covers only specific separate encounters. Furthermore, prioritize XE or other X modifiers when filing a claim to Medicare.

XE Modifier and Related Modifiers
---------------------------------

X(EPSU) is a cluster of four Healthcare Common Procedure Coding System (HCPCS) modifiers. These codes were introduced to reduce the complexity of modifier 59.

*   **XP**– It applies to a separate physician giving the service for a distinct procedure.
*   **XS**– It applies to a separate structure for a distinct service. A separate structure means a different organ.
*   **XU**– It covers the usual non-overlapping services. It avoids overlapping of the components of the leading service.

Common Mistakes to Avoid
------------------------

Before filing a claim to the payer, you must avoid the following XE modifier in medical billing mistakes.

*   Using modifier 59 instead of XE in medical billing leads to claim rejection.
*   The modifier accepts X modifiers, so don’t use modifier 59. It will lead to claim denials.
*   Overuse of modifiers can cause the practice to undergo audits.
*   Applying modifiers to the wrong code.

Hence, use modifiers appropriately and don’t overuse modifiers. Avoiding these common mistakes can optimize the overall financial performance.

Conclusion
----------

To sum up, the XE modifier in medical billing is among the subsets of X modifiers of HCPCS. A provider receives reimbursement for the distinct services performed on the same day but at different times. Likewise, applying the XE modifier to the medicare payer is crucial. Other payers may reject the XE modifier as they prefer modifier 59. In addition, avoid mistakes like applying the modifier to the wrong code and using modifier 59 instead of XE. Hence, avoiding these mistakes and accurately submitting claims protects the bottom line.
