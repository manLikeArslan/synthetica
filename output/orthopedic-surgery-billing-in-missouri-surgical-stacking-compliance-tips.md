What Is Surgical Stacking and Why It Matters?
---------------------------------------------

Surgical stacking refers to billing multiple procedures during the same surgical session. When a patient undergoes bilateral knee replacements, rotator cuff repair plus subacromial decompression, or ankle fusion combined with ligament reconstruction, you’re stacking surgical procedures.

The complexity arises because insurance companies have strict rules about which procedures can be billed separately and which must be bundled as a single charge. Medicare uses the Physician Fee Schedule (PFS) and specific bundling rules. Missouri-based commercial insurance carriers have their own bundling edits and billing requirements. Federal regulations, particularly those around the Anti-Kickback Statute and Stark Law, add additional compliance considerations.

**Here’s what makes surgical stacking so critical:** a single incorrectly stacked procedure can result in a claim denial, demand letter, or audit. When patterns emerge across multiple claims, insurance companies may investigate billing practices more broadly. In worst-case scenarios, this leads to recoupments—where the insurance company demands repayment of previously paid claims—or compliance reviews by the Office of Inspector General (OIG) or state medical board.

For orthopedic surgeons in Missouri, the stakes are high. Your reputation, your reimbursement, and your practice’s financial stability depend on getting surgical billing right.

Understanding Missouri-Specific Surgical Billing Rules
------------------------------------------------------

Missouri doesn’t have state-specific surgical stacking rules that override Medicare or federal guidelines, but Missouri-based insurance carriers do have their own requirements. Anthem Blue Cross Blue Shield of Missouri, for example, has distinct bundling rules that differ from Medicare’s approach. Cigna, United Healthcare, and other carriers operating in Missouri each have unique surgical policies.

This creates a complex landscape where a procedure that bundles under Medicare might be billable separately under a commercial plan—and vice versa. Many Missouri orthopedic practices don’t realize that their billing approach works for Medicare but violates commercial carrier rules, leading to selective denials and audit triggers.

Additionally, Missouri’s state regulations require billing practices to be transparent and fully documented. The Missouri Board of Healing Arts, which oversees physician practices, expects complete accuracy in billing submissions. When billing errors occur—even unintentional ones—they can affect your standing with the board.

Understanding these nuances is essential. What works in neighboring Kansas or Illinois won’t necessarily work in Missouri. Your billing must be tailored specifically to Missouri insurance requirements and state regulations.

Key Surgical Stacking Compliance Tips for Orthopedic Surgeons
-------------------------------------------------------------

### 1\. Know Your Bundling Rules by Carrier

Don’t assume all insurers follow the same bundling rules. Medicare has its bundling edit system, but Anthem, Cigna, UnitedHealthcare, and other Missouri carriers have their own. Create a reference document for each major carrier you work with, documenting which procedures bundle and which are separately billable. Update this quarterly as carriers release new policies.

Your billing team should never submit a claim without confirming the specific bundling rules for that carrier and procedure combination.

### 2\. Document Your Surgical Procedures Comprehensively

Your operative report is your primary documentation. It must clearly describe each procedure performed, the anatomical site, the specific techniques used, and the clinical justification for each component. When procedures are separate and distinct—requiring different surgical approaches, incisions, or clinical reasoning—this documentation becomes critical evidence that you’ve coded correctly.

Incomplete documentation is one of the leading causes of surgical stacking denials. Insurance companies use operative reports to verify that multiple procedures were indeed necessary and distinct. A vague operative report creates opportunity for denial.

### 3\. Use Proper Modifiers for Multiple Procedures

Modifier 51 indicates a multiple procedure. Modifiers 76 and 77 indicate repeat procedures. Modifiers LT and RT denote left and right sides. Using correct modifiers is non-negotiable. Incorrect modifier usage is one of the most common surgical billing errors and frequently triggers denials or automatic bundling by insurance claim processing systems.

Your billing software should have rules built in to flag potential modifier errors before submission. Manual review of high-value claims by a human coder prevents costly mistakes.

### 4\. Understand Bilateral Procedure Rules

Bilateral procedures (performed on both sides of the body) have specific billing requirements. Medicare typically pays 150% of the allowable amount for bilateral procedures, with specific guidelines about when bilateral services can be billed as two separate claims versus one combined claim.

Missouri commercial carriers often have different bilateral rules. Some require a bilateral modifier on a single claim. Others allow two separate claims with appropriate modifiers. Billing bilateral procedures incorrectly is extremely common and results in predictable denials.

### 5\. Watch for Add-On Codes and Package Pricing

Orthopedic procedures often include add-on codes—procedures that can only be billed in conjunction with a primary procedure. Attempting to bill an add-on code without its associated primary code triggers automatic denials. Conversely, some orthopedic procedures are considered package pricing, where multiple components must be billed together as one unit.

Understanding which procedures are add-ons versus standalone procedures is fundamental to surgical compliance.

### 6\. Implement Pre-Operative Authorization and Pre-Billing Review

Before surgery, secure pre-authorization from the patient’s insurance carrier whenever you’re planning to stack procedures. This documentation serves two purposes: it clarifies what the insurance company considers billable, and it protects your practice by creating a record that the procedures were pre-approved.

After authorization but before claim submission, have your billing team conduct a final compliance review. This second set of eyes catches errors before they result in denials.

### 7\. Track Denials and Establish a Systematic Appeals Process

Not all denials are legitimate. Many result from claim processing errors or misunderstandings by insurance carriers. Systematic tracking of denials—particularly patterns in surgical stacking denials—reveals whether your billing approach is compliant or whether you need to adjust coding practices.

Denied claims should be appealed with supporting documentation, particularly operative reports and clinical justification for procedures. Appeals often succeed when proper documentation is provided.

Common Surgical Stacking Mistakes in Orthopedic Billing
-------------------------------------------------------

Many Missouri orthopedic practices make preventable mistakes that trigger denials and audit risk. Understanding these errors helps you avoid them.

*   **Unbundling procedures that should be packaged**: Some orthopedic surgeons bill arthroscopic procedures and subsequent open surgical repair as separate procedures when they should be combined into a single claim. This is considered unbundling and violates billing compliance rules.
*   **Incorrect bilateral procedure coding**: Billing bilateral knee replacements as two separate procedures with identical codes, rather than using bilateral modifiers or carrier-specific bilateral protocols, results in automatic denials.
*   **Using outdated CPT codes**: Orthopedic surgery has regular CPT code updates. Using old codes creates confusion, denials, and compliance risk. Your billing system should be updated annually with new CPT codes.
*   **Inadequate operative documentation**: Operative reports that don’t clearly justify each procedure component create vulnerability during audits. Insurance companies and auditors assume that inadequately documented procedures weren’t medically necessary.
*   **Bundling procedures that should be separate**: Conversely, some practices bundle procedures that are legitimately billable separately, leaving significant revenue on the table.
