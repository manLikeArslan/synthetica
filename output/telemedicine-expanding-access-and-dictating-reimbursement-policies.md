**The Reimbursement Red Tapes**
-------------------------------

Along with each state, laws must dictate if and how doctors may provide tele-health services. By 2017, nearly every state codified the process to establish a valid doctor-patient relationship via tele-health and prevent payers from mandating an in-person visit before tele-health use. But the types of procedures that patients are eligible for and the payment structure vary from state to state.

Gradually, states implement pay parity laws that require payers to reimburse tele-health procedures to the same extent as in-person services.

From January 2017, it is mandatory for 31 states and the District of Columbia to have private payer parity and 20 states and D.C. require Medicaid parity.

The states of Alaska and Arizona are the only states that impose partial parity, where all the services are not covered. In Arizona, legislators have come to add services on a piecemeal basis.

Coverage of telemedicine consultation services for trauma, burn, cardiology, infectious diseases, mental health disorders, neurologic diseases, and dermatology took effect on Jan. 1, 2015.

Pulmonology services were added in 2016, and this year, the Arizona Urological Society (AZUS) led a campaign to include urology to the list of telemedicine services that must be given insurance payment parity.

FAQs
----

****1\. How does telemedicine affect healthcare providers’ revenue cycle and ROI?****

Telemedicine introduces new opportunities for revenue growth but also presents challenges, such as navigating varying reimbursement policies and ensuring compliance with medical billing and coding regulations. Providers must adapt to these changes to prevent revenue stagnation and protect ROI.

****2\. Are telemedicine services reimbursed on par with in-person services?****

Reimbursement varies by state. As of 2017, 31 states and the District of Columbia have private payer parity, requiring telemedicine services to be reimbursed at the same rate as in-person visits. However, states like Alaska and Arizona enforce partial parity, covering only specific telemedicine services.

****3\. What are the major challenges for telemedicine adoption in outpatient care?****

The biggest challenges include confusion around reimbursement, physician liability, and medical billing and coding ethics. The lack of uniform legislation across states exacerbates these issues, making it difficult for providers to streamline telemedicine practices.

****4\. Are patients and physicians ready for the transition to telemedicine?****

Yes, a significant percentage of both groups are ready. About 66% of urology patients are willing to participate in telemedicine, and physician adoption of telemedicine services rose to 71% in 2017 from 54% in 2014.

****5\. What is the role of state laws in telemedicine services?****

State laws dictate how and if telemedicine services can be provided, including the procedures eligible for reimbursement and the payment structures. States are gradually implementing pay parity laws, but the lack of consistency in regulations continues to pose challenges for healthcare providers and patients.
